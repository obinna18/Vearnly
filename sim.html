Project Overview

Solutions In Me (FUPRE Edition) is a student innovation challenge run at the Federal University of Petroleum Resources, Effurun. Sponsored by Aliva Tech & Gadgets, students are challenged to identify real problems in campus and daily life, design practical solutions, and compete for a ₦45,000 prize pool split across three placements.

I served as volunteer graphic designer across all four campaign phases, from the initial teaser before any announcement was made, through the educational tip series, the event announcement, and the submission-open phase. 9 distinct pieces, one unified brand, zero design budget.

The Problem

Student innovation challenges at Nigerian universities typically get announced with a single low-quality flyer posted in a WhatsApp group. The post gets 12 views, three "wow"s, and zero submissions. The event happens, nobody shows up, the organizer blames student apathy.

The real failure is design and communication failure, not apathy. Students need to encounter an idea multiple times, across multiple touchpoints, before they take it seriously. A single flyer is not a campaign. It is a missed opportunity.

The brief: Build a design campaign that makes every FUPRE student aware of Solutions In Me, makes the concept of identifying a problem feel achievable, and drives actual submissions before the deadline.

The solution was a four-phase campaign architecture: generate curiosity before the reveal, educate the audience on what the challenge requires, build social proof through visibility, then close with a clear submission CTA. Each phase needed its own design language while remaining unmistakably part of one campaign.

Brand System

The core brand device is the torn-edge navy banner. It appears in every single piece across all 9 flyers and is the primary visual element that makes the campaign identifiable at a glance in any student's feed or WhatsApp chat.

The banner creates a two-zone composition across every piece: the navy zone holds primary campaign messaging (the challenge name, tip number, key CTA), and the white zone above and below holds the logo, sponsor credit, hashtag, and supporting information. This structure kept layout consistent across flyers with very different information densities — a teaser needs almost no information, a submission-open flyer needs five distinct information categories. The banner handled both without the layout falling apart.

Design rule: Every piece has the torn-edge banner. Every piece has the SIM logo in the same position. Every piece ends with the sponsor credit and hashtag. Everything else can change — photography, text, colours in secondary zones — as long as those three elements hold.

Color Palette

Two primary colors. No gradients, no accent neutrals, no softening. The palette is high-contrast by design.

#FF6600

#FFFFFF
Secondary zones · Body text on navy · Logo zone backgrounds

Why this palette? Navy communicates authority, institutional seriousness, and a premium feel that separated this campaign from typical campus designs built on stock gradients and bright green. Orange against navy is one of the highest-contrast pairings possible — key information lands clearly even at thumbnail size in an Instagram grid, even on a cracked phone screen, even on a printed A4 posted on a notice board.

The constraint was also intentional. Two colors are easier to maintain consistently across 9 pieces produced over multiple weeks. Campaigns that introduce too many colors in later phases drift. Two colors cannot drift.

Typography

Headlines: Bold condensed sans-serif (Lato Bold / Montserrat Bold Condensed). Condensed type packs authority into limited horizontal space, allowing large, readable text without crowding the composition. All campaign headlines needed to read at thumbnail size before the viewer decides to tap. Condensed letterforms make that possible at larger point sizes.

Body text and labels: Lighter weight of the same family. One typeface, two weights. The contrast between bold condensed headlines and regular-weight supporting text creates clear hierarchy without introducing the visual complexity of a second typeface. Simpler to manage across a multi-week campaign, and the hierarchy reads instantly.

Exception — the teaser flyer: A handwritten script font was used for the single word "Anticipate..." on the Phase 1 teaser. This was the only departure from the campaign's condensed-sans rule, and it was deliberate. The contrast between the structured brand logo and the flowing personal script creates a human moment inside a formal brand. That tension is what a teaser needs. It should feel personal, not polished.

Campaign Phases

Phase 1: Teaser (1 flyer)

Full-bleed navy. No information, no deadline, no CTA. Just the wordmark and one script-font word: "Anticipate..." The goal was pure curiosity. Restraint creates more curiosity than information does. Students who saw this flyer had no idea what was coming — that uncertainty is the entire point of a teaser.

Phase 2: Educational Tip Series (5 flyers)

A parent card ("How to Identify a Problem") followed by four numbered method cards: Look Around You, Listen to Complaints, Spot Frustration, Check the Crowd. The oversized number at the base of each card (1, 2, 3, 4) was the most deliberate single decision in the series. On Instagram, a large number at the base of a card signals: there is a series and this is where it starts. It primes the viewer to look for the next one.

Phase 3: Event Announcement (1 flyer)

Light background signals a content shift — this is no longer educational content, it is a call to action. The campaign logo comes to the foreground. Functions as a reset and a reveal: all the tips were building toward this specific event.

Phase 4: Submission Open (1 flyer)

The densest information piece in the campaign. Five categories: how to submit, what your solution must be, the selection process, the deadline, and the prize amount. Two-column card layout with clearly labelled sections. The deadline was pulled out in orange bold at the bottom — it is the single piece of information that determines whether a student acts or loses the opportunity.
